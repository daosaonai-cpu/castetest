What is Machine Learning?

Machine Learning is a subset of Artificial Intelligence that focuses on building systems that learn from historical data to make predictions or decisions.

Traditional Programming

  • We give a computer a set of rules (code) and data.
  • The computer processes the data and outputs the answer.
  • Think of how a standard calculator operates.
Example: Writing an IF/THEN script to flag any transaction over $10,000 as suspicious.

Machine Learning

  • We give the computer the data AND the answers.
  • We ask the computer to figure out the rules.
  • The machine learns to perform tasks from experience (data) without explicit programming.
Example: Feeding the computer thousands of past transactions (some labeled 'fraud', some 'normal'). The computer figures out the subtle patterns that indicate fraud on its own.

Module 1: The Three Branches of ML

1. Supervised Learning

Labeled Data: The algorithm is trained on a dataset that already has the answers.

The Goal: Learn a mapping function from input variables (features) to the output variable (target/label).

Prediction: Once trained, the model can accurately predict outputs for brand new, unseen inputs.

Classic Example: Predicting house prices from square footage, or classifying emails as Spam/Not Spam.

2. Unsupervised Learning

Unlabeled Data: The algorithm learns from raw data. There are no predefined answers or labels provided.

The Goal: Discover the underlying structure, hidden patterns, or groupings within the dataset autonomously.

Classic Example: Customer segmentation (grouping buyers by behavior) or Anomaly detection (spotting credit card fraud).

3. Reinforcement Learning

Feedback Loop: An "agent" learns to behave in an environment by performing actions and seeing the results.

The Goal: Maximize a "reward" signal over time through trial and error.

Classic Example: Training an AI to play chess, or teaching a self-driving car to stay in its lane (reward = staying on road, penalty = crashing).

Module 2: Foundational ML Algorithms

Linear Regression (Supervised)

Used when you need a continuous numerical value as an answer (e.g., price, temperature).

It tries to draw the best possible straight line through your data points. It uses Ordinary Least Squares to minimize the 'error' (distance between the line and actual points).

The classic algebraic equation for a line: y = mx + c

Real World: Predicting a person's adult height (y) based on their current age (x).

Logistic Regression (Supervised)

Despite the name, it's used for binary classification. It fits an S-shaped curve that predicts the probability (between 0 and 1) that an input belongs to a specific class.

Real World: Predicting if a patient has a specific disease (1 = Yes, 0 = No) based on their blood test results.

Decision Trees (Supervised)

A visual tool that automatically figures out the best sequence of Yes/No questions to ask in order to split your data into the most 'pure' groups possible at the leaf nodes.

Real World: A bank deciding whether to approve a loan. (Question 1: Income > $50k? -> Question 2: Credit Score > 700? -> Approve).

K-Means Clustering (Unsupervised)

  1. Choose 'K': You define the number of clusters (groups) you want to find.
  2. Drop Centroids: The algorithm drops 'K' random center points into the data.
  3. Assign: Every data point is assigned to its nearest centroid.
  4. Recalculate: The center of the new group is calculated, and the centroid moves there. This repeats until movement stops.

The Goldilocks Problem: Underfitting vs. Overfitting

A major challenge in training models is finding the perfect balance. We want our model to learn the underlying trend, not just memorize the data.

Underfitting (Too Simple)

The model is too basic and fails to capture the underlying pattern of the data. It performs poorly on both training and new data.

Analogy: A student who didn't study at all and fails the test.

Optimal (Just Right)

The model captures the true underlying trend without memorizing the noise. It generalizes well to unseen data.

Analogy: A student who understands the core concepts and can apply them to new questions.

Overfitting (Too Complex)

Trees are prone to overfitting. They can become too complex and perfectly memorize the training data, leading to terrible performance on brand new, unseen data.

Analogy: A student who memorized the exact practice questions but fails when the real test changes the numbers.

Module 3: Model Evaluation & Metrics

Training and Testing Data

The Beginner's Trap: Testing a model on the same data it was trained on is like giving a student the answer key before a test. It ruins our ability to know if the machine actually learned anything useful.

Train / Test Split: We typically hide 20% of our data (Testing Set) and build the model with the remaining 80% (Training Set). We then test it on the hidden data to evaluate its ability to generalize.

Cross-Validation: A rigorous technique where data is split into multiple chunks. The model is trained and tested multiple times on different chunk combinations to get a robust average score.

Regression Metrics (Numbers)

When predicting numerical values, we look at distances:

  • Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Straightforward. On average, how far off were our predictions from reality?
  • R-squared: Indicates how much of the variance in the target is explained by our model. Higher is better.

Classification Metrics (Categories)

When predicting categories, simple accuracy can be misleading, especially with imbalanced data.

  • Precision: When we predict a positive, how often are we right? (Minimizes false alarms).
  • Recall (Sensitivity): Out of all actual positives, how many did we find? (Minimizes misses).
  • F1-Score: A mathematical balance (the harmonic mean) between Precision and Recall. It is the best metric to use when you need a balance between the two and your dataset is uneven.
